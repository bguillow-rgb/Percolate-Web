Coffee-to-Water Ratio: The Right Numbers for Every Brew

By Percolate · Published June 22, 2026

Quick Answer

The standard coffee-to-water ratio is 1:16, 1 gram of coffee per 16 grams of water, for drip and pour-over. French press works best closer to 1:15, espresso at 1:2, and cold brew concentrate at 1:4 to 1:5. Weigh in grams for consistent results; adjust by 1 gram at a time to dial in your taste.

Getting your coffee-to-water ratio right is the single fastest way to improve every cup you brew. It matters more than upgrading your kettle, switching filter papers, or obsessing over pour patterns, because no technique can rescue a brew that started with the wrong dose. This guide gives you exact numbers for every common method, explains what the ratio is actually doing to your flavor, and shows you how to adjust intelligently when something tastes off.

What exactly is a coffee-to-water ratio, and why does it matter so much?

The brew ratio is simply the weight of ground coffee you use compared to the weight of water you pour over it. A 1:16 ratio means 1 gram of coffee for every 16 grams of water. Scale it up: 20 grams of coffee to 320 grams of water, 30 grams to 480 grams. The relationship stays constant and the math is easy.

Why weight and not volume? Because coffee density shifts with every variable you change. A tablespoon of light-roast coffee weighs meaningfully more than a tablespoon of dark roast, and a tablespoon of coarse grounds weighs less than the same scoop of medium grind. Volume measurements introduce inconsistency before you even touch your kettle. A basic kitchen scale that reads to the nearest gram removes all of that variability and makes your brew repeatable every single morning.

The ratio matters because it governs how many soluble flavor compounds end up in your cup. Pour too much water relative to your coffee dose and you dilute those compounds, producing a thin, sour result. Use too little water and you over-concentrate them, pushing the cup toward harshness and bitterness. The goal is to land inside what the Specialty Coffee Association calls the ideal extraction window.

What does the SCA Golden Ratio actually mean for a home brewer?

A question we hear often: people see “1:18” cited everywhere and wonder if that’s the number they should be targeting.

The SCA Golden Cup Standard recommends 55 grams of coffee per liter of water, approximately a 1:18 ratio, targeting a brew strength of 1.15, 1.35% Total Dissolved Solids and an extraction yield of 18, 22%. That extraction window matters: research by the Specialty Coffee Association has shown that extraction yields between 18% and 22% correspond to the range most drinkers describe as balanced and pleasant, while below 18% the cup turns sour and thin, and above 22% it tips into bitterness.

Here’s the catch that trips up a lot of home brewers: the Golden Cup Standard was originally designed for large-batch commercial brewing, the kind of auto-drip machine running multiple liters at a cafe counter. At smaller single-cup volumes, the same 1:18 ratio often produces a brew that feels underpowered, with less body and aroma than you’d expect from good beans. Most specialty coffee drinkers brewing at home find the sweet spot sits closer to 1:15 to 1:17, depending on method and roast level. Think of 1:18 as the floor, not the target.

What’s the right ratio for each brew method?

Every method extracts coffee differently. Contact time, filter type, pressure, and grind size all shift how aggressively water pulls flavor from the grounds. A ratio that tastes perfect in a pour-over can taste flat in a French press, or aggressive in an AeroPress. Here’s a method-by-method reference:

Brew MethodRatio RangeNotes
Pour-Over (V60, Chemex, Kalita)1:15 - 1:17Start at 1:16 for medium roast; pull tighter for light roast
Drip / Auto-Drip1:16 - 1:17Medium-ground; check your carafe’s actual cup size (usually 5-6 oz, not 8)
French Press1:12 - 1:15Full immersion; metal filter passes oils, so a slightly stronger dose balances body
AeroPress1:12 - 1:16Wide range by design; shorter brew = tighter ratio, bypass dilution = loosen it
Espresso1:218g in, ~36g out in 25-30 seconds
Cold Brew Concentrate1:4 - 1:5Steep 16-18 hours; dilute 1:1 before serving
Cold Brew (Ready-to-Drink)1:12 - 1:15No dilution needed; brew directly to drinking strength

For drip and pour-over at the standard 1:16 ratio, a practical landmark: 30 grams of coffee to 480 ml of water makes about a 16-oz travel mug’s worth of coffee. That’s a useful anchor to memorize and scale from.

Why does my coffee still taste sour even when I follow the ratio?

This one comes up a lot.

Sour, thin-tasting coffee is almost always a sign of under-extraction. The water didn’t pull enough flavor compounds out of the grounds. The ratio is one lever, but it works alongside two others: grind size and brew time. You can have a correctly weighted ratio and still under-extract if your grind is too coarse (water passes through without enough contact time) or your water temperature is too low.

The practical troubleshooting order: first, confirm your ratio is at or tighter than 1:16. If the sourness persists, grind slightly finer before you change anything else. Finer grounds expose more surface area and extract faster. Water temperature is the third dial: brew water should be 195-205 degrees F (90-96 degrees C) for most methods. Below 195 degrees F, extraction slows significantly and sour notes dominate. Only after you’ve verified those three variables should you experiment with further adjusting the ratio.

Conversely, bitter and harsh coffee almost always points to over-extraction. Loosen your grind before you dilute your ratio. Over-extraction is usually a contact-time problem, not a dose problem.

Should I change my ratio based on roast level?

Readers frequently ask: whether the numbers in a brewing guide apply equally to a light Kenyan and a dark Sumatra sitting side by side on their coffee shelf.

They don’t, and the difference is real enough to matter. Light roasts are denser and more structurally intact; they extract more slowly and often need a tighter ratio (closer to 1:15) to yield the fruit-forward, complex flavors they’re known for. Dark roasts are more porous. The extended roasting process breaks down cellular structure, so they give up solubles faster. At a ratio as tight as 1:15, a dark roast can easily tip into bitterness. Starting at 1:16 or even 1:17 gives you more margin before that threshold.

The same logic applies when you notice a bag tasting unexpectedly flat. Before deciding the beans are stale or the roaster let you down, try tightening the ratio by 1-2 grams of coffee per 250 ml of water. A light roast that seems muted at 1:17 often snaps into focus at 1:15. Logging your ratio alongside your tasting notes, something the Percolate cupping log makes easy to track per bag, helps you build a personal data set of what works for each coffee you own.

How do I convert grams into tablespoons without a scale?

A scale is the most reliable tool, but if you’re starting without one, here are workable landmarks: one level tablespoon of medium-ground coffee weighs approximately 5 grams; a standard coffee scoop (two tablespoons) weighs roughly 10 grams. That puts a 1:16 ratio at about 2 tablespoons per 6-oz cup, which is the widely cited starting-point recommendation.

The caveat: volume-based measuring drifts as roast level and grind size change. A tablespoon of coarse French press grind will weigh less than a tablespoon of medium drip grind. A tablespoon of light-roast whole-bean coffee weighs more than a tablespoon of dark roast because light roasts are denser. If you want consistent cups, invest in a basic kitchen scale. They’re inexpensive and eliminate all of this guesswork in one step.

How do I fix a brew that tastes too weak or too strong?

The adjustments are simple, but the direction trips people up. To make coffee stronger, add more coffee. Do not reduce water. Brewing with less water increases extraction pressure and can make the cup harsh rather than stronger in a pleasant way. To make coffee weaker, reduce coffee dose, not brew time. Extending brew time without adjusting dose causes over-extraction, which adds bitterness rather than lightness.

A clean adjustment workflow:

  1. Tastes thin and sour? Tighten ratio by 2-3g more coffee. If that doesn’t help, also grind slightly finer.
  2. Tastes bitter and harsh? Loosen ratio by 2-3g less coffee. If persistent, coarsen your grind first.
  3. Tastes flat and uninteresting? Check bean freshness first (most specialty coffee is at its best 7-30 days past roast date), then tighten ratio.
  4. Tastes right but inconsistent day to day? Switch to weight-based measuring if you haven’t already.

Shift one thing at a time and taste after each adjustment. This is the same iterative approach cuppers use, and it’s the reason structured tasting notes are worth keeping. When you’re working through multiple bags at once, a coffee shelf app like Percolate lets you attach brew notes directly to each bag so you remember what ratio worked best on that specific coffee.

Does water quality change the effective ratio?

Yes, though most home brewers underestimate this. Water with high mineral content, particularly high alkalinity, can make a correctly-rationed brew taste flat and muted, because certain minerals interfere with how acids and aromatic compounds dissolve. Very soft or distilled water strips the cup of body. The SCA recommends water with balanced mineral content for optimal flavor extraction. If your tap water is noticeably hard or chlorinated, filtered or lightly mineralized bottled water often produces a noticeably cleaner cup at the same ratio. A useful reminder that ratio optimizes what’s already in the water, but doesn’t fix the water itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the golden ratio for coffee?

The SCA Golden Cup Standard recommends 55 grams of coffee per liter of water, roughly a 1:18 ratio, targeting a balanced extraction yield of 18-22% and 1.15-1.35% TDS. In practice, most specialty coffee brewers find 1:15 to 1:17 produces a richer, more satisfying cup at home, especially with single-origin or light-roast beans.

How much coffee should I use per cup?

For a standard 6-oz cup, use about 10-11 grams of coffee (roughly 2 level tablespoons) at a 1:16 ratio. For a larger 12-oz mug, use 20-22 grams. A digital scale gives you far more consistency than tablespoons, since a scoop of light roast weighs more than the same scoop of dark roast.

Does coffee-to-water ratio affect flavor, or just strength?

Both. Ratio primarily controls strength, but it also interacts with extraction. Too much water (1:20+) can leave coffee tasting sour and thin because not enough solubles dissolve. Too little water (below 1:13) can turn bitter and harsh. The ratio sets the ceiling; grind size and brew time fine-tune the balance within that ceiling.

Should I use a different ratio for light versus dark roast?

Yes. Light roasts are denser and extract more slowly, so a tighter ratio like 1:15 helps pull more flavor from the bean. Dark roasts are more porous and dissolve faster, so they generally work well at 1:16 to 1:17. Going too tight on a dark roast amplifies bitterness quickly.

Do I really need a scale, or can I use tablespoons?

A scale is strongly recommended. Coffee density varies significantly based on roast level and grind size. A tablespoon of light-roast coffee weighs more than a tablespoon of dark roast. Measuring in grams eliminates that variability and makes your brew repeatable every morning. A basic kitchen scale that reads to the nearest gram is all you need.

What ratio should I use for cold brew?

Cold brew concentrate uses a 1:4 to 1:5 ratio, roughly 100 grams of coarse-ground coffee per 400-500 ml of room-temperature water, steeped for 16-18 hours, then diluted 1:1 before serving. For ready-to-drink cold brew that doesn't need diluting, use a 1:12 to 1:15 ratio instead.
