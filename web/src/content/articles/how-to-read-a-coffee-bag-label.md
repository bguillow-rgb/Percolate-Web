Pick up almost any specialty coffee bag and you'll find a small wall of text that most people skip entirely, origin, process, masl, varietal, a date that may or may not be the roast date. For casual shoppers it's background noise. For home brewers who buy multiple bags and want to understand what they're drinking, it's one of the most useful documents in the kitchen. This guide breaks down every field, in the order that matters most.

## What's the single most important thing to check on a coffee bag?

*A question we hear often:* the roast date, and it's not particularly close.

The roast date tells you when the green coffee was put through the roaster. It's the freshness clock, and it governs almost everything about how the coffee will taste. Most specialty roasters stamp it on the bag because they're proud of their freshness; commodity brands tend to hide it behind a vague best-by date.

After roasting, beans release carbon dioxide in a process called degassing. Brew too early and excess CO₂ interferes with extraction, producing an uneven, gassy cup. Wait too long and the aromatic compounds responsible for those nuanced flavors have faded. For most filter brew methods, pour-over, drip, French press, the practical window is **7 to 21 days post-roast**. Espresso benefits from a bit more rest, typically **10 to 30 days**, because pressure brewing amplifies any CO₂-related harshness. Beyond 45-60 days, most coffees taste noticeably flat regardless of how well the bag was sealed.

A best-by date is a food-safety marker, usually set 12-24 months after roasting. Coffee can be perfectly safe to drink and still taste stale and unremarkable because the aromatics have long since gone. If a bag shows only a best-by date with no roast date, you have no way of knowing how fresh the coffee actually is, that's a meaningful transparency gap.

## How do I decode the origin information, country, region, farm?

Origin is listed in ascending order of specificity, and the more specific it is, the more the roaster trusts their supply chain.

| Origin Level | Example | What It Tells You |
|---|---|---|
| Country only | Colombia | Broad regional flavor profile; common in blends |
| Country + Region | Colombia, Huila | Much more specific; Huila is high-altitude and known for sweet, balanced cups |
| Country + Region + Farm | Colombia, Huila, Finca La Palma | Gold standard; you know exactly who grew it and where |
| Cooperative / Mill | Ethiopia, Yirgacheffe, Kochere Mill | Multiple small producers pooled at one processing station |

The country name sets broad flavor expectations. Ethiopian coffees are frequently associated with floral, tea-like brightness; Guatemalan coffees tend toward balance and spiced notes; Colombian coffees typically land in the sweet-to-bright range depending on region. But country alone is a loose guide. Regional and farm-level specificity is what actually predicts the cup.

When a cooperative or mill name appears instead of a single farm, it usually means many small-plot farmers deliver their harvests to a central processing station. That's not a quality red flag, some of the most celebrated Ethiopian lots come from cooperative mills, but it does mean the coffee represents multiple contributors rather than a single producer's work.

## What does the processing method tell me before I brew?

*This one comes up a lot:* the processing method, washed, natural, or honey, is arguably the single biggest flavor driver listed on the bag, and it's one most buyers skip.

Processing describes how the seed (what we call the coffee bean) was removed from the fruit after harvest. The method transfers different amounts of fruit sugar, fermentation character, and acidity into the final bean before it ever reaches a roaster.

| Process | What Happens | Flavor Result |
|---|---|---|
| Washed (Wet) | Fruit fully removed before drying | Clean, bright, origin-forward; acidity is clear and distinct |
| Natural (Dry) | Whole cherry dries in the sun with fruit intact | Fruity, wine-like, sometimes funky; fruit sugars absorb into the bean |
| Honey | Skin removed but sticky mucilage left on to dry | Sweet, balanced, body between washed and natural |
| Anaerobic / Extended Ferment | Sealed fermentation vessel before drying | Experimental; intense, sometimes tropical or boozy character |

A washed Ethiopian and a natural Ethiopian from the same farm can taste so different that they seem like completely separate origins. Learning to pair process with your preferred flavor direction is one of the fastest ways to stop buying bags you don't enjoy. If naturals feel too intense or fermented, look for washed or honey. If washed coffees seem too lean, try a natural.

For a deeper breakdown of each method, see our guide to [washed vs. natural vs. honey processing](/washed-vs-natural-vs-honey-process).

## What does the altitude (masl) number actually predict about the cup?

Masl stands for meters above sea level, the elevation at which the coffee trees were grown. It appears because altitude has a direct and well-documented effect on flavor complexity: at higher elevations, cooler temperatures slow the development of the coffee cherry, giving sugars and aromatic compounds more time to accumulate in the seed.

As a working guide:
- **Below 1,000 masl**, softer, milder cups; low acidity
- **1,200-1,500 masl**, solid specialty range; more developed sweetness and acidity
- **1,500-1,800 masl**, where most exciting specialty coffees live
- **Above 1,800 masl**, exceptional; this is where competition-level lots are often sourced

Yemen, for context, grows coffee between approximately 1,500 and 2,500 masl, some of the highest-grown coffee in the world, which partly explains the extraordinary complexity of Yemeni lots. Ethiopia's Yirgacheffe region regularly produces coffee at 1,900-2,100 masl, a factor directly linked to its signature jasmine and citrus character.

Altitude is useful for comparing two coffees from the same country or region. It shouldn't be used in isolation, a low-altitude coffee grown by a meticulous producer on ideal soil can outperform a high-altitude lot that was poorly processed. But if a bag lists the altitude, that's already a signal you're holding a roaster who cares about traceability. Our guide to [how coffee growing altitude shapes flavor](/coffee-growing-altitude-flavor-guide) goes much deeper on the science.

## What is a coffee varietal, and does it matter for the average home brewer?

*Readers frequently ask:* whether varietal information is worth paying attention to, or just specialty jargon.

A coffee varietal is the specific subspecies of coffee plant from which the beans were harvested, the equivalent of grape varieties in wine. Within Arabica (the species used for virtually all specialty coffee), there are thousands of distinct varieties, each with different flavor potential, disease resistance, and yield characteristics. When a roaster prints the varietal on the bag, they're signaling it genuinely influences what you'll taste.

Some varietals to know by name:

| Varietal | What to Expect |
|---|---|
| Bourbon | Foundational variety; prized for sweetness, complexity, and balance |
| Typica | The genetic ancestor of many others; clean, delicate, classic cup |
| Gesha (Geisha) | Intensely floral, tea-like, jasmine and tropical fruit; routinely sets auction records |
| Catuai | Productive and reliable; bright acidity, common in Central America |
| SL28 / SL34 | Kenyan classics; bold blackcurrant and citrus character |
| Heirloom / JARC | Ethiopian wild or semi-wild varieties; enormously diverse and often spectacular |

For the average home brewer buying their first few specialty bags, varietal matters less than roast date, roast level, and processing method. But once you start comparing two washed Colombians or two natural Ethiopians and wondering why they taste different, the varietal is often the answer. Roasted coffee contains over 1,200 volatile flavor compounds, and genetic heritage is one of the factors that determines which of those compounds end up in your cup.

## How do I use roast level and tasting notes together to predict what I'll like?

*A question we hear often:* whether the listed tasting notes are accurate, or just marketing.

Tasting notes on specialty coffee bags describe naturally occurring flavor compounds that developed during growing, processing, and roasting. When a bag says "blueberry" or "bergamot," nothing was added to the coffee, the beans contain volatile chemical compounds that trained tasters perceive as resembling those flavors. Roasters determine tasting notes through professional cupping sessions where multiple people taste the coffee blind, record what they perceive independently, and the most common descriptors become the official notes.

That said, tasting notes are a promise, not a guarantee. Whether you perceive them depends on brew method, grind precision, water quality, and palate experience. Treat the notes as a directional signal rather than a literal flavor list. "Stone fruit, brown sugar, milk chocolate" tells you to expect a sweet, mellow, medium-bodied cup. "Jasmine, grapefruit, black tea" tells you to expect something bright, aromatic, and delicate.

Roast level adds the final layer of prediction:
- **Light roast**, origin flavors are most preserved; acidity is most pronounced; tasting notes tend to be fruit- and flower-forward
- **Medium roast**, balance point; sweetness comes forward, acidity softens
- **Dark roast**, roast character dominates; origin nuance recedes; expect chocolate, caramel, and toasty notes regardless of origin

For brew method pairing: light-to-medium roasts tend to shine in pour-over and drip, where clean water and paper filters let origin flavors stand out. Darker roasts perform well in French press and espresso, where body and boldness are the point. See our [coffee roast levels guide](/coffee-roast-levels-explained) for the full breakdown.

## What do certifications like Fair Trade, Organic, and Direct Trade actually guarantee?

Certifications sit in a separate category from the flavor and freshness fields above, they're ethical and economic labels, not taste predictors.

**Fair Trade** ensures farmers received at least the Fair Trade minimum price (around $1.80/lb for Arabica as of recent standards) and that certain labor and environmental conditions were met. It does not guarantee taste quality. A Fair Trade coffee can range from disappointing commodity-grade to excellent specialty-grade.

**Organic** means the coffee was grown without synthetic pesticides or fertilizers. It addresses environmental and health concerns but says nothing about cup quality, an organically grown coffee can still be poorly processed, poorly roasted, or stale.

**Direct Trade** is not a third-party certification at all. It's a roaster's self-reported claim that they sourced directly from the producer, bypassing commodity markets. No external body verifies it. Many specialty roasters who practice genuine direct trade are more transparent about pricing and farm relationships than certified brands, but the term on its own requires trusting the roaster's word.

The practical hierarchy: **roast date → origin → process → roast level** will predict your cup. Certifications matter for ethical purchasing decisions, but they don't tell you whether the coffee will taste good.

## What does a genuinely great specialty bag label look like in practice?

Imagine picking up a bag and reading: *Ethiopia / Yirgacheffe / Kochere Washing Station / Heirloom varieties / Washed / 1,900-2,100 masl / Light roast / Roasted: June 22, 2026 / Notes: jasmine, bergamot, lemon verbena.*

You now know the coffee is from a famous high-altitude Ethiopian micro-region, processed at a respected cooperative mill using the washed method (expect clarity and brightness), grown above 1,900 masl (expect complexity), roasted light (origin flavors front and center), roasted 17 days ago (right in the filter-brew sweet spot), and that a trained cupper perceived floral and citrus notes when evaluating it. You haven't tasted a drop yet and you already have a confident, well-informed picture.

That's what a specialty bag label is supposed to do. The more fields present and the more specific each one is, the more the roaster trusts their product. Vague labels, *"100% premium Arabica, smooth and rich"*, are hiding something, whether that's a missing roast date, an unknown origin, or processing details the roaster would rather not share.

Reading labels well is a skill that compounds quickly. Log your bags, roast date, origin, process, your own tasting impressions, and patterns emerge fast. You'll discover that you consistently reach for washed East Africans at light roast, or that honey-processed Colombians match your morning routine perfectly.
