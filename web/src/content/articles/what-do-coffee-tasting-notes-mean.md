Pick up any bag of specialty coffee and you'll find something unexpected printed on it: *blueberry*, *dark chocolate*, *jasmine*, *brown sugar*, *stone fruit*. If you're new to specialty coffee, or even if you've been buying it for years, those words can feel more like poetry than practical information. This guide explains what they actually mean, where they come from, and how to use them to buy better coffee every time.

## Wait, does my coffee bag actually have blueberry in it?

*A question we hear often:*

No, and this is the single most important thing to understand about specialty coffee tasting notes. Unless a bag is explicitly labeled as "flavored coffee" (think: hazelnut, French vanilla, pumpkin spice), those descriptors are not added ingredients. When you read "blueberry" on a bag of natural-processed Ethiopian coffee, it means the bean itself contains aromatic compounds that share chemical characteristics with blueberry. Nothing was ever mixed in.

Coffee is one of the most chemically complex beverages we consume. Sensory research puts the count of aromatic and flavor compounds in coffee at over 800, more than wine, each contributing to its overall character. These molecules develop in the cherry as it ripens on the tree, transform during processing, and shift again during roasting. The result is a range of flavors that can legitimately remind trained and untrained tasters alike of fruits, flowers, nuts, spices, or chocolate, all without a single additional ingredient.

This is why specialty coffee labels read so differently from commercial grocery-store bags. Commercial blends are typically roasted dark and blended to flatten variability; their flavor is predictable roast character. Specialty coffee is roasted to highlight what's already in the bean, which is why the notes on the bag matter, and why they can genuinely surprise you.

## Where do those flavor compounds actually come from?

The flavors you read on a bag trace back to at least four distinct stages of a coffee's journey from farm to shelf:

**Origin and terroir.** Altitude, soil composition, rainfall, and average temperature all shape what flavor precursors develop inside the coffee cherry. High-elevation coffees grown above 1,800 meters, common in Ethiopia and Kenya, tend to develop more complex acids that translate as fruit and floral notes in the cup. Lower-altitude beans, like many from Brazil, develop more lipids and carbohydrates that read as chocolate and nut.

**Variety.** Just as Cabernet Sauvignon and Pinot Noir taste different despite both being red wine grapes, coffee varieties like Gesha, Bourbon, and Typica each carry their own flavor potential. A Gesha from Panama, for example, is widely associated with jasmine and peach notes regardless of where it's grown, because of its genetics. (See our guide to [coffee varieties](https://percolateapp.com/articles/coffee-varieties-explained) for a deeper breakdown.)

**Processing method.** How a coffee is dried after harvest has a massive effect on its sweetness, fruit intensity, and fermented character. Washed coffees tend to taste clean and bright; natural-processed coffees are typically fruitier and heavier-bodied; honey-processed coffees fall somewhere in between. Our article on [washed vs. natural vs. honey processing](https://percolateapp.com/articles/washed-vs-natural-vs-honey-process) explains exactly how these differences show up in the cup.

**Roast level.** A light roast preserves delicate origin character, fruit, florals, bright acidity. As the roast gets darker, Maillard reactions and caramelization progressively replace origin flavors with roast-derived ones: chocolate, caramel, smoke, and ultimately ash. This is why you'll almost never see a specialty roaster put "blueberry" on a dark roast bag.

## How does the roaster actually decide what words to put on the bag?

*Readers frequently ask:*

It's more rigorous than it might seem. Specialty roasters evaluate every coffee through a structured **cupping session**, a standardized tasting protocol where coffees are prepared identically, tasted by multiple people, and scored on aroma, flavor, sweetness, acidity, body, and finish. Each taster writes down every sensory impression independently. Then the group compares notes. The words that appear most consistently across tasters become the bag descriptors.

This process is anchored by the **SCA Coffee Taster's Flavor Wheel**, first published in 1995 and substantially revised in 2016 by the Specialty Coffee Association in collaboration with World Coffee Research. The updated wheel contains 110 sensory attributes, more than doubling the original 52. It was developed by professional sensory panelists, researchers, and coffee industry experts who evaluated 105 coffee samples from 13 countries and spent over 100 hours building a science-backed vocabulary. When a roaster writes "raspberry" on a bag instead of just "berry," they're using that shared language to be as precise as possible.

The wheel works concentrically: broad categories like *fruity*, *floral*, *nutty/cocoa*, and *roasted* sit in the center; more specific descriptors like *stone fruit* or *dark chocolate* occupy the middle ring; and the most granular notes, *apricot*, *bittersweet chocolate*, *jasmine*, sit on the outer edge. When you see three descriptors on a bag, think of them as three points on that wheel sketching the profile of what's inside.

## What do the most common tasting note categories actually taste like?

Here's a practical reference for the flavor families you'll encounter most often on specialty coffee bags:

| Note Category | Typical Sensory Experience | Often Found In |
|---|---|---|
| **Citrus** (lemon, orange, grapefruit) | Bright, lively, tangy acidity; tart sparkle on the tongue | Washed Kenyans, light-roasted Ethiopians |
| **Berry** (blueberry, raspberry, strawberry) | Sweet-tart fruit, sometimes with a hint of jam or ferment | Natural Ethiopians, some Yemenis |
| **Stone Fruit** (peach, apricot, plum) | Softer acidity, gentle sweetness, smooth mouthfeel | Honey-processed Guatemalans, Colombians |
| **Floral** (jasmine, rose, chamomile) | Delicate aromatic lift; perfumed finish | Gesha varieties, light-roasted Ethiopians |
| **Chocolate** (dark, milk, cocoa) | Rich, roasted sweetness; lower acidity; heavier body | Brazilian naturals, medium-dark roasts |
| **Nutty** (almond, hazelnut, peanut) | Mild, earthy sweetness; rounded finish | Brazilian and Sumatran coffees |
| **Caramel / Brown Sugar** | Gentle sweetness, smooth; mellow acidity | Medium-roasted Colombians, Hondurans |
| **Spice** (cinnamon, clove, cardamom) | Warm, slightly sharp aromatic notes | Some Yemenis, Indonesian coffees |
| **Roasted / Smoky** | Bold, dry, sometimes bitter character | Dark roasts across most origins |

One important nuance: flavors in coffee are *impressions*, not literal ingredients. When tasters describe blueberry, they mean the coffee activates similar olfactory receptors to blueberry, not that it tastes exactly like eating one. The comparison helps your brain locate the right sensory memory. With practice, those connections get faster and more precise.

## Why don't I always taste what the bag promises?

*This one comes up a lot:*

Several variables between the bag and your cup can shift, or mute, the notes you're expecting.

**Temperature is the biggest one.** Many fruit and floral compounds are volatile and most perceptible as the coffee cools from around 65 degrees C down to 45 degrees C. Drink it very hot and you may only pick up broad roast character; let it cool slightly and the subtler notes open up. Our guide to [brew water temperature](https://percolateapp.com/articles/brew-water-temperature-guide) covers how temperature also affects extraction.

**Grind size and extraction** matter enormously. An under-extracted coffee (too coarse a grind, too short a brew) will taste sour and thin, fruit notes read as sour rather than bright. An over-extracted coffee goes bitter and flat. Dialing in your grind for your brew method is the fastest way to access what's on the label. See our [grind size guide](https://percolateapp.com/articles/coffee-grind-size-by-brew-method) for specifics.

**Freshness** plays a significant role too. As roasted coffee ages, volatile aromatic compounds dissipate. A blueberry note that was vivid on day seven after roast may be nearly gone by week six. This is why specialty roasters print a roast date rather than an expiration date, and why buying fresh matters. (How long to rest coffee after roasting matters just as much, covered in our guide on [how long to rest coffee after roasting](https://percolateapp.com/articles/how-long-to-rest-coffee-after-roasting).)

**Your personal taste memory** is also a factor. If you've never eaten a fresh lychee, that note is genuinely harder to identify than, say, dark chocolate. This isn't a failing, it just means your palate improves as you accumulate more reference points. Tasting two coffees with contrasting profiles side by side is one of the fastest ways to sharpen your ability to perceive specific notes.

## How should I actually use tasting notes when buying coffee?

Tasting notes work best as a navigation tool, not a guarantee. Once you know you respond well to chocolatey, nutty coffees, you can scan for those descriptors on a new bag. If you've been gravitating toward citrusy and floral cups, you now know to look for light-roasted washed coffees from Ethiopia or Kenya.

The smarter habit is to track your reactions *after* brewing. Note what you actually tasted, whether it matched the bag, and whether you enjoyed it. Over several bags, patterns emerge: you might discover that you love stone-fruit notes but find "fermented" or "winey" characters overwhelming. That knowledge is far more useful than any roaster's label.

## Can I train myself to taste more in my cup?

Yes, and it's genuinely faster than most people expect. Research on sensory training consistently shows that regular focused tastings, using structured reference tools, comparing coffee to real-world foods, and paying deliberate attention to aroma improve flavor recognition over weeks rather than months.

The most practical way to start: brew two coffees side by side from contrasting origins, say, a natural Ethiopian alongside a washed Colombian. Before you sip, smell each one. Then taste, letting the coffee coat your whole tongue rather than swallowing immediately. Ask yourself the broadest question first: is this more fruity, more chocolatey, or more roasted? Then narrow inward, using the flavor wheel or the tasting notes on each bag as a reference. You're not trying to match the bag perfectly; you're building a sensory library, note by note.

Aroma is your sharpest tool. Because smell accounts for the vast majority of our flavor perception, training your nose, on the dry grounds before brewing, on the bloom, and in the cup, develops your palate faster than any other single habit. Don't skip the sniff.
